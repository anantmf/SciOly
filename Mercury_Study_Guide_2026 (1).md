## 1. BASIC INFORMATION & OVERVIEW

**Quick Facts:**
- **Position in Solar System:** Closest planet to the Sun
- **Classification:** Terrestrial planet (rocky planet)
- **Named After:** Roman messenger god due to its swift movement across the sky
- **Visibility:** Best observed near sunrise or sunset; challenging to spot due to proximity to Sun
- **Key Distinction:** Despite being closest to the Sun, it is NOT the hottest planet (Venus is)
- **Density Ranking:** Second densest planet after Earth

Mercury is often described as a planet with a "Moon-like" exterior and "Earth-like" interior due to its Moon-sized crust combined with a large metallic core similar to Earth's.

## 2. ORBITAL CHARACTERISTICS

### Orbital Period and Motion
- **Orbital Period:** 87.969 Earth days (shortest orbital period of any planet)
- **Orbital Speed:** Average 47.36 km/s (fastest orbital speed of any planet)
- **Maximum Speed:** 58.97 km/s at perihelion
- **Minimum Speed:** 38.86 km/s at aphelion

### Orbital Distances
- **Semimajor Axis (Average Distance):** 57.909 million km (0.387 AU)
- **Perihelion (Closest to Sun):** 46.0 million km (0.307 AU)
- **Aphelion (Farthest from Sun):** 69.818 million km (0.459 AU)
- **Orbital Variation:** 23.8 million km difference between closest and farthest points

### Orbital Eccentricity
- **Eccentricity Value:** 0.2056
- **Significance:** Most eccentric orbit of all planets; this high eccentricity significantly affects Mercury's rotation and illumination patterns
- **Comparison:** Earth's eccentricity is 0.0167, making Mercury's orbit much more elliptical

### Orbital Inclination
- **Inclination to Ecliptic:** 7.004 degrees
- **Significance:** Highest inclination of the terrestrial planets; this creates a tilted orbital plane relative to other planets

### Special Orbital Feature: 3:2 Spin-Orbit Resonance
- Mercury completes exactly 1.5 rotations for every complete orbit around the Sun
- This means Mercury's "year" is only about twice as long as its "day"
- Creates unique illumination patterns on the surface
- Results in "hot" poles (at certain longitudes) and "warm" poles (at other longitudes)

## 3. PHYSICAL PROPERTIES & SIZE

### Size Comparisons
- **Equatorial Radius:** 2,440.5 km
- **Comparison to Earth:** 38.3% of Earth's diameter
- **Comparison to Earth's Moon:** Slightly larger than the Moon
- **If scaled:** Three Mercurys placed end-to-end would slightly exceed Earth's diameter
- **Surface Area:** 75 million square kilometers (approximately 10% of Earth's surface area)

### Mass and Density
- **Mass:** 0.33010 × 10²⁴ kg (5.53% of Earth's mass)
- **Density:** 5,429 kg/m³ (second highest; only slightly less than Earth's 5,513 kg/m³)
- **Why so dense?** Extremely large iron-rich core with thin rocky crust

### Surface Gravity
- **Surface Gravity:** 3.70 m/s² (38% of Earth's gravity or 0.38g)
- **Escape Velocity:** 4.3 km/s (38.4% of Earth's escape velocity)
- **Weight Comparison:** A person weighing 100 kg on Earth would weigh approximately 38 kg on Mercury

### Shape
- **Ellipticity (Flattening):** 0.0009 (essentially spherical, not significantly flattened at poles)
- **Polar vs. Equatorial Radius:** Nearly identical (2,438.3 km vs. 2,440.5 km)

## 4. INTERNAL STRUCTURE & COMPOSITION

### Core Structure (The Defining Feature)
Mercury's internal structure is dominated by an unusually large metallic core:

**Core Dimensions:**
- **Core Radius:** Approximately 1,289 km (about 1,300-2,000 km in total estimates)
- **Core as Percentage of Planet:** 
  - By radius: ~53% of Mercury's radius
  - By volume: 42% of Mercury's volume
  - By mass: 70-80% of Mercury's total mass
- **Comparison to Earth:** Earth's core is only ~17% of its volume; Mercury's core is 2.5 times more massive relative to planetary size

**Core Composition:**
- Primarily iron (Fe) and nickel (Ni)
- May contain silicon (Si), sulfur (S), and carbon (C)
- Possibly a layer of iron sulfide (FeS) at core-mantle boundary (controversial)

**Core Structure (Multi-layered):**
1. **Solid Inner Core:** 
   - Radius: ~1,089-1,200 km
   - Solid iron composition
   - Similar size to Earth's inner core despite Mercury being much smaller
   - Confirmed by NASA research in 2023

2. **Liquid Outer Core:**
   - Surrounds the solid inner core
   - Evidence suggests it is molten or partly molten
   - Responsible for generating Mercury's magnetic field through dynamo action

### Mantle
- **Thickness:** Approximately 500-700 km from core to crust boundary
- **Composition:** Silicate rock, similar to Earth's mantle
- **Characteristics:** 
  - Chemically heterogeneous (non-uniform composition)
  - Suggests Mercury underwent a magma ocean phase early in its history
  - Contains layers with large-scale variations in chemical composition
  
### Crust
- **Thickness:** 16-35 km (estimates vary)
- **Composition:** 
  - Low in iron compared to mantle
  - High in sulfur
  - Silicate rocks (similar to Earth's continental crust)
  - Denser than typical Earth crustal material
- **Characteristics:**
  - Formed largely through volcanic activity (magmatic crust)
  - More dense than aluminum
  - Shows evidence of earlier impact basin influences

### Overall Internal Layering
From surface to center:
1. Thin silicate crust (~35 km)
2. Large silicate mantle (~500-700 km)
3. Liquid iron-rich outer core (~1,200 km radius)
4. Solid inner core (~1,100 km radius)

## 5. SURFACE FEATURES & TOPOGRAPHY

### Major Surface Feature Categories

#### 1. Impact Craters
- **Prevalence:** Most common feature on Mercury's surface
- **Characteristics:** Unlike lunar craters due to Mercury's higher surface gravity
- **Key Difference from Moon:** Mercury's higher gravity (twice that of Moon) means:
  - Material ejected from craters travels only ~65% of the distance as on the Moon
  - Complex crater features (central peaks, slumped walls) occur in smaller craters
  - Minimum crater diameter for complex form: ~10 km (vs. ~19 km on Moon)

**Notable Impact Basins and Craters:**
- **Caloris Basin (Caloris Planitia):**
  - Largest known crater on Mercury
  - Diameter: 1,550 km (960 miles)
  - Surrounded by concentric mountain ring ~2 km tall
  - Floor filled with distinctive smooth plains
  - Shows polygonal pattern of ridges and fractures
  - Origin: Massive impact that may have triggered volcanic eruptions
  - Visual: Double-ringed impact basin structure

- **Multiring Basins:**
  - Structures 300+ km in diameter
  - Result from most energetic impacts
  - Several dozen recognized on imaged portion of Mercury
  - Provide evidence of intense early asteroidal bombardment

#### 2. Intercrater Plains
- **Distribution:** Comprise ~70% of Mercury's surface in examined regions
- **Characteristics:**
  - Oldest visible surface features
  - Gently rolling or hilly plains between larger craters
  - Show scarcity of craters below ~30 km diameter
  - Appear to have covered up or destroyed many earlier craters
- **Origin:** Uncertain - possibly volcanic or impact-related
- **Significance:** Uniformly distributed across entire planet surface

#### 3. Smooth Plains
- **Resemblance:** Similar to lunar maria (dark flat regions)
- **Composition:** Likely volcanic in origin
- **Distribution:** Fill interior of Caloris Basin and other impact basins
- **Characteristics:** More recent than intercrater plains

#### 4. Rugged Highlands
- **Characteristics:** Resemble corresponding regions on the Moon
- **Origin:** Result of early impact bombardment

#### 5. Lobate Scarps (Thrust Faults)
- **Definition:** Steep-sided ridges formed by thrust faulting
- **Appearance:** One-sided, often lobate (lobe-shaped), appearing as curved ridges
- **Distribution:** Linear or arcuate segments; some arc for over 1,000 km
- **Origin:** Formed by horizontal compression as Mercury's crust cooled and contracted globally
- **Timing:** Formed after the period of heavy bombardment (~4 billion years ago); younger than intercrater plains

**Notable Scarps:**
- **Discovery Rupes:**
  - Most notable observed by MESSENGER
  - Stretches over 550-600 km
  - Relief: ~1.5 km high
  - One of the most curved lobate scarps on Mercury

- **Adventure Rupes & Resolution Rupes:**
  - Part of an arcuate trend over 1,000 km long
  - May be topographically continuous, representing single thrust fault
  - Heights: 1.3 km and 0.9 km respectively

- **Beagle Rupes:**
  - Among longest known scarps
  - Intersects Sveinsdóttir crater (one of largest features affected by scarp)

#### 6. Hilly and Lineated Terrain ("Weird Terrain")
- **Location:** Antipodal (opposite side) to Caloris Basin
- **Characteristics:** Unique jumbled, fractured terrain
- **Possible Origin:** Seismic waves from the giant Caloris impact focused on opposite hemisphere, disrupting and jumbling the surface
- **Significance:** Suggests Mercury's interior transmitted tremendous seismic energy

### Topographic Range
- **Total Range:** Approximately 7 km from lowest to highest point
- **Comparison to Earth:** Earth's topographic range is ~20 km

## 6. ATMOSPHERE (EXOSPHERE)

### Definition and Characteristics
- **What It Is:** Extremely thin surface-bounded exosphere, NOT a true atmosphere
- **Pressure:** Less than ~5 × 10⁻¹⁵ bar (0.005 picobar or ~10⁻¹⁴ bar)
- **Comparison:** More vacuum than can be readily produced in Earth laboratories
- **Density:** ~10 quadrillion (10¹⁶) times thinner than Earth's atmosphere at sea level

### Why So Thin?
Several factors prevent Mercury from retaining a substantial atmosphere:
1. **Low Gravity:** Mercury's small mass and surface gravity (0.38g) cannot hold gas molecules
2. **Extreme Heat:** Close proximity to Sun heats surface to extreme temperatures, accelerating gas molecules beyond escape velocity
3. **Solar Wind:** Intense solar wind pressure "blows away" atmospheric particles
4. **Lack of Magnetic Protection:** While Mercury has a weak magnetic field, it's insufficient to protect the exosphere

### Composition of Mercury's Exosphere

**Primary Components (from Solar Wind):**
- Hydrogen (H): ~230 cm⁻³
- Helium (He): Intermediate concentration
- Believed to originate from solar wind capture

**Secondary Components (from Planetary Surface):**
- Oxygen (O): ~44,000 cm⁻³ (highest concentration; likely from crust)
- Sodium (Na): Column density ~1 × 10¹¹ cm⁻²
- Potassium (K)
- Calcium (Ca)
- Magnesium (Mg)
- Silicon (Si)
- Hydroxide (OH)
- Possible trace amounts of: Water (H₂O), Argon (Ar), Carbon Dioxide (CO₂), etc.

### Mercury's Atmospheric Tail
- **Feature:** Comet-like tail of escaping gas extending away from planet opposite to Sun
- **Cause:** Solar wind pressure pushes exospheric particles away
- **Components in Tail:** Sodium, calcium, and other heavier atoms visible in tail

### Exosphere Dynamics
- **Highly Variable:** Composition and density fluctuate significantly
- **Transient Nature:** Atoms enter and leave constantly
- **Continuous Loss:** Mercury constantly loses exospheric gases to space
- **Continuous Replenishment:** Solar wind and surface processes continuously provide new atoms

### Key Source Mechanisms
1. **Solar Wind Implantation:** Hydrogen and helium from solar wind become trapped in magnetic field
2. **Micrometeorite Sputtering:** Micrometeoroids vaporize when striking surface, releasing elements (especially calcium)
3. **Photochemical Processes:** Solar ultraviolet radiation can break down surface materials
4. **Radioactive Decay:** Some helium may originate from decay of uranium in crust

## 7. TEMPERATURE EXTREMES

### Surface Temperature Ranges
- **Daytime Maximum:** 
  - Equatorial noon temperatures: Up to 700 K (427°C or 800°F)
  - At specific longitudes during perihelion: Even higher (~700-725 K)
- **Nighttime Minimum:** 
  - As low as 100 K (-173°C or -280°F)
  - Can reach below 112 K (-161°C) at poles
- **Total Range:** Over 800 K difference between day and night (among most extreme of any planet)

### Internal Temperatures
- **Core-Mantle Boundary:** ~1,380 K on the sunward side
- **Surface Average:** ~440 K (167°C)

### Why Such Extreme Temperature Variation?

1. **Lack of Atmosphere:** 
   - Mercury's virtually non-existent exosphere cannot conduct heat around planet
   - No greenhouse effect
   - No circulation of air to distribute heat

2. **Slow Rotation:**
   - Mercury rotates very slowly (58.6 Earth days per rotation)
   - One side faces Sun for long periods, becoming extremely hot
   - Other side faces away, becoming extremely cold
   - Takes 176 Earth days for one full Mercury "day-night cycle"

3. **High Orbital Eccentricity:**
   - 3:2 spin-orbit resonance creates special "hot poles" and "warm poles"
   - At certain longitudes, local noon coincides with perihelion (closest approach)
   - Resulting in "hot poles" reaching even higher temperatures

4. **Highly Reflective Surface:**
   - Some areas reflect solar radiation differently due to composition
   - Creates local variations in temperature

### Diurnal (Day-Night) Temperature Variation Pattern
At Mercury's equator:
- From less than 100 K during night
- To up to 700 K at local noon
- Variation influenced by Mercury's 3:2 spin-orbit resonance

## 8. MAGNETIC FIELD

### Basic Properties
- **Strength:** Approximately 1.1% of Earth's magnetic field strength
- **Equatorial Strength:** ~300 nanoTesla (nT) at Mercury's equator
- **Configuration:** Approximately a magnetic dipole field
- **Global Nature:** Apparently global in extent

### Discovery and Measurement
- **Discovery Date:** 1974 by Mariner 10 spacecraft
- **Measurement:** First spacecraft to measure Mercury's magnetic field
- **MESSENGER Refinements:** Recent observations confirmed North-South asymmetry

### Origin of Magnetic Field
- **Source:** Dynamo action within Mercury's liquid outer core
- **Composition:** Iron-rich liquid core generates field similar to Earth's mechanism
- **Efficiency Issue:** Field is weaker than theoretical expectations based on core size

### Why So Weak?
Several hypotheses explain Mercury's surprisingly weak magnetic field:

1. **Core Cooling:** 
   - Mercury's core cooled and solidified more rapidly than Earth's
   - Faster cooling reduced dynamo efficiency

2. **Feedback Dynamo Model:**
   - Interactions between variable external magnetic field and Mercury's core
   - Interplanetary magnetic field affects core dynamo efficiency
   - Results in self-limiting field strength

3. **Small Size:**
   - Mercury's smaller size meant faster cooling history
   - Reduced time for dynamo operation

### Magnetosphere
- **Definition:** Region of space dominated by Mercury's magnetic field
- **Function:** Deflects solar wind particles around planet
- **Bow Shock:** Magnetic field strong enough to create bow shock (deflection point) in solar wind
- **Comparisons:** 
  - Stronger than Jupiter's moon Ganymede's field
  - But still only ~1% of Earth's field

### Interaction with Solar Wind
- **Solar Wind Pressure:** Three times larger at Mercury's orbit than at Earth's orbit
- **Magnetosphere Size:** Smaller than Earth's due to weaker field and increased solar wind pressure
- **Result:** Mercury's magnetosphere is compressed close to planet surface

## 9. GEOLOGICAL HISTORY & FORMATION

### Planet Formation Context
Mercury was formed ~4.6 billion years ago during the early Solar System's formation from the solar nebula.

### Formation Theories

**Traditional Model - Accretion:**
- Mercury formed in situ from planetesimals and protoplanetary material
- Core-mantle differentiation occurred (dense iron sank to center)
- Should have resulted in smaller core than currently observed

**Giant Impact Hypothesis (Preferred):**
- Mercury was originally a larger planet with more normal core-mantle ratio
- Suffered a massive collision with a Mars-sized planetesimal early in history
- The impact stripped away much of Mercury's silicate mantle and crust
- Left behind the large iron core surrounded by thin rocky shell
- Explains Mercury's abnormally large core

**Planetary Migration Context (Nice Model):**
- Mercury likely formed closer to current position but may have shifted slightly
- Giant planets' migration during Late Heavy Bombardment may have scattered asteroids toward inner planets
- Would explain intense cratering visible on Mercury's surface

### Early History - Heavy Bombardment
- **Period:** First ~700-900 million years after formation
- **Evidence:** Heavily cratered terrain and intercrater plains
- **Cause:** Intense asteroid and meteorite impacts as solar system cleared
- **Result:** Created most visible craters and impact basins observed today

### Late Planetary Evolution
- **Volcanic Activity:** Continued after heavy bombardment period
- **Formation of Smooth Plains:** Volcanic eruptions created smoother plains filling impact basins
- **Tectonism:** Lobate scarps formed as planet cooled and contracted globally
- **Gradual Cooling:** Planet has been cooling since formation, causing contraction
- **Recent Activity:** Some lobate scarps show evidence of activity even after smooth plains deposition

### Time Periods (Mercurian Geological Time Scale)
- **Pre-Tolstojan:** Oldest terrain; predates most recognizable features
- **Tolstojan:** Period of intercrater plains formation
- **Calorian:** Period of Caloris Basin formation and surrounding geology
- **Mansurian:** Period after Caloris Basin formation
- **Kuiperian:** Most recent period; characterized by relatively young craters

## 10. POLAR ICE DEPOSITS

### Discovery
- **Initial Hypothesis:** Proposed decades ago; seemed impossible on close-to-Sun planet
- **First Evidence (1991):** Arecibo radio telescope detected radar-bright patches at poles
- **Confirmation (2012):** NASA's MESSENGER spacecraft confirmed water ice presence

### Characteristics of Polar Deposits

**Location and Distribution:**
- Found in permanently shadowed polar craters
- Both north and south poles contain deposits
- Mercury's low axial tilt (~0.034°) maintains permanent shadows near poles

**Composition:**
- **Primary Constituent:** Water ice (H₂O) is major component
- **Overlying Material:** Dark material covers most deposits
- **Dark Material Origin:** Likely complex organic compounds from comets and volatile-rich asteroids
- **Exposed Ice:** In coldest deposits, ice exposed at surface

**Quantity Estimates:**
- If deposits spread over area size of Washington, D.C., would be more than 2 miles (3.2 km) thick
- Distributed across multiple north and south polar deposits

### Why Ice Exists on Mercury?

**Temperature Requirements:**
- Permanently shadowed regions maintain temperatures around -170°C (-280°F)
- Cold enough for water ice stability despite Mercury's proximity to Sun
- Hydrogen content measurements indicate nearly pure water ice in buried layers
- Surface temperatures slightly too warm where ice is buried (~slightly above -170°C)

**Formation Hypothesis:**
- Ice likely delivered by comets and volatile-rich asteroids
- Same objects that brought water to early Earth
- Accumulated over billions of years
- Protected in perpetually shadowed craters from solar heating

### Scientific Significance
- **Surprising Discovery:** Mercury contains water despite being closest planet to Sun
- **Inner Solar System Water:** Challenges understanding of water distribution in early solar system
- **Resource Implications:** Future exploration might consider these as potential resources
- **Evolutionary History:** Suggests complex delivery mechanisms for volatile compounds

### Observation Methods
- **Neutron Spectrometer:** Measured hydrogen content indicating water ice presence
- **Mercury Laser Altimeter (MLA):** Provided topographic maps showing shadowed regions
- **Thermal Modeling:** Surface and subsurface temperature models confirmed ice stability
- **Radar Observations:** Ground-based radar remained key identification tool

## 12. SPACE EXPLORATION & MISSIONS

### Mariner 10 (1974-1975)
- **Mission Type:** Flyby mission
- **Historic Significance:** First spacecraft to visit Mercury
- **Close Approaches:** Three flybys
- **Accomplishments:**
  - Discovered Mercury's magnetic field
  - Imaged less than 50% of planet surface
  - Mapped major surface features including Caloris Basin
  - Measured temperature extremes
  - First detailed surface observations
- **Limitations:** Could not map entire planet due to orbital mechanics

### MESSENGER (Mercury Surface, Space ENvironment, GEochemistry, and Ranging) - 2011-2015
- **Mission Type:** Orbital mission
- **Duration:** Over 4 years in orbit (March 2011 to April 2015)
- **Accomplishments:**
  - Determined Mercury's surface composition
  - Revealed geological history
  - Discovered details about internal magnetic field
  - Verified polar deposits are dominantly water ice
  - Provided detailed high-resolution surface maps
  - Completed global mapping of Mercury
  - Gathered extended atmospheric measurements
- **End:** Mission concluded when spacecraft ran out of fuel and impacted Mercury's surface
- **Legacy:** Provided unprecedented understanding of Mercury

### BepiColombo (2018-2026)
- **Mission Type:** International orbiter mission
- **Partners:** European Space Agency (ESA) and Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA)
- **Launch Date:** October 20, 2018
- **Composition:** Three main components:
  1. Mercury Transfer Module (MTM) - provides propulsion
  2. Mercury Planetary Orbiter (MPO) - European component (11 instruments)
  3. Mercury Magnetospheric Orbiter (MMO/MIO) - Japanese component
- **Journey:** Eight-year journey from launch to Mercury orbit
- **Flybys:** Completed 9 flybys; last flyby January 8, 2025
- **Planned Orbit Insertion:** November 2026
- **Scientific Instruments Include:**
  - MERTIS spectrometer (developed by German Aerospace Center)
  - BELA (laser altimeter)
  - Various composition and magnetic field instruments
- **Observations Already Made:** First detailed thermal infrared views of Mercury's surface

### Key Mission Differences
| Aspect | Mariner 10 | MESSENGER | BepiColombo |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------|
| Type | Flyby | Orbit | Orbit |
| Duration at Mercury | Brief flybys | 4+ years | Expected multi-year |
| Coverage | <50% of surface | Global mapping | Global mapping + depth |
| Magnetic Field | Discovered field | Detailed measurements | More precise measurements |
| Composition | Basic | Detailed | Very detailed |
| Polar Ice | Not confirmed | Confirmed | Further investigation |

## 13. KEY CONCEPTS FOR SCIENCE OLYMPIAD COMPETITION

### Comparison Questions - Mercury vs. Other Planets/Bodies

**vs. Earth:**
- Density: Nearly equal (Mercury slightly lower)
- Size: Mercury ~38% Earth's diameter
- Core ratio: Mercury's core is 42% of volume (Earth's is 17%)
- Gravity: Mercury 38% of Earth's
- Atmosphere: Mercury has exosphere only; Earth has thick atmosphere
- Magnetic field: Mercury 1.1% of Earth's field

**vs. Moon:**
- Size: Mercury slightly larger
- Density: Mercury much higher (rocky vs. Earth-rocky-core)
- Craters: Mercury's gravity makes complex craters form smaller
- Magnetic field: Mercury has field; Moon does not (currently)
- Interior: Mercury has large metallic core; Moon has small core
- Temperature range: Mercury more extreme

**vs. Venus:**
- Temperature: Venus hotter on average despite farther from Sun (greenhouse effect)
- Atmosphere: Venus thick; Mercury thin exosphere
- Surface gravity: Similar
- Density: Similar

### Calculation and Problem-Solving Areas

**Orbital Calculations:**
- Time to orbit = 87.97 Earth days
- Orbital velocity from Kepler's laws
- Synodic period relative to Earth (~115.88 days)

**Temperature Conversions:**
- Kelvin ↔ Celsius ↔ Fahrenheit
- Example: 700 K = 427°C = 800°F
- Example: 100 K = -173°C = -280°F

**Scale Comparisons:**
- Mercury radius to Earth radius ratios
- Core radius to total radius ratios
- Crater size comparative calculations

**Density Calculations:**
- Using mass and volume
- Mercury: 0.33010 × 10²⁴ kg ÷ 6.083 × 10¹⁰ km³
- Compare to Earth and other planets

### Important Definitions - Student Must Know

- **Exosphere:** Extremely thin, surface-bounded atmosphere; essentially a vacuum
- **Lobate Scarp:** Thrust fault creating curved ridge from planetary contraction
- **Spin-orbit Resonance:** Orbital and rotational period locked in ratio (3:2 for Mercury)
- **Eccentricity:** Measure of orbit's elliptical shape (Mercury: 0.206)
- **Perihelion:** Closest point in orbit to Sun
- **Aphelion:** Farthest point in orbit from Sun
- **Dynamo:** Mechanism generating magnetic field from liquid outer core motion
- **Multiring Basin:** Large impact structure with multiple concentric rings
- **Complex Crater:** Large impact crater with central peak(s) and slumped walls
- **Intercrater Plains:** Ancient terrain between larger impact craters
- **Weird Terrain:** Hilly, jumbled terrain created by seismic waves from Caloris impact

## PRACTICE QUESTIONS

**True/False:**
1. Mercury has the hottest daytime temperatures of any planet in the solar system. (False - Venus does)
2. Mercury's core comprises less than 20% of its volume. (False - ~42%)
3. Mercury has a thin exosphere rather than a true atmosphere. (True)
4. Mercury's rotation period is shorter than its orbital period. (False - rotation is 58.6 days; orbit is 87.97 days)
5. Water ice exists at Mercury's poles. (True)
